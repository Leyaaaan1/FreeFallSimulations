Free Fall Formulas & Equations Reference

Core Free Fall Equations (No Air Resistance)

In a vacuum, all objects fall identically regardless of mass. These are the fundamental kinematic equations every physics student needs.

d = ½ × g × t²  ← distance (m)
v = g × t  ← velocity (m/s)
t = √(2d / g)  ← fall time (s)
v² = 2 × g × d  ← velocity² shortcut

d = distance (m)  ·  g = gravity (m/s²)  ·  t = time (s)  ·  v = velocity (m/s)

With Air Resistance (Drag Equation)

Real-world free fall includes atmospheric drag. This simulator uses numerical integration to solve these equations each 0.005s time-step.

Fnet = mg − ½ρCdAv²
a = Fnet / m
vt = √(2mg / ρCdA)  ← terminal velocity
KE = ½mv²  ← kinetic energy (J)

ρ = air density  ·  Cd = drag coefficient  ·  A = cross-section area

Key Physics Concepts Explained

🎯

What is Free Fall?

Free fall is motion under gravity alone with no other forces. In a vacuum, every object — a feather or a bowling ball — falls at identical acceleration. Galileo proved this at the Tower of Pisa around 1589.

🌬️

What is Terminal Velocity?

When drag force equals gravitational force, net acceleration reaches zero and the object falls at constant speed. A human skydiver reaches ~53 m/s (190 km/h) in a belly-to-earth position. A raindrop reaches ~9 m/s.

📐

Does Mass Affect Free Fall?

In a vacuum: no — Galileo's equivalence principle. With air resistance: yes — heavier objects reach higher terminal velocities because drag is proportionally smaller relative to gravitational force.

⚡

Kinetic Energy at Impact

KE = ½mv² tells you the energy transferred at impact. A 1 kg ball dropped 100 m on Earth hits with ~981 J — equivalent to a small explosion. On Jupiter the same drop produces ~2,479 J due to stronger gravity.

🪐

Why Jupiter Falls Fastest

Jupiter's surface gravity of 24.79 m/s² is 2.53× Earth's. Objects accelerate over 2.5× faster, reaching 70+ m/s after just 100 m in vacuum — but Jupiter's atmosphere limits real velocities significantly.

🔢

Numerical Integration Method

This simulator uses Euler integration at Δt = 0.005 s (200 steps/sec), recalculating velocity and position with updated drag every step. This gives accurate results even when drag changes with velocity.
